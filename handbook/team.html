Organizing and preparing your team: Continuous improvement of web content

On this page

  • Team composition
  • Organizational alignment

Team composition

Web content improvement projects usually require the participation of 4 groups:

  • improvement core team
  • usability research specialists
  • subject matter experts
  • senior management and decision makers

Improvement core team

The improvement core team is responsible for coordination, design, content and interaction. The primary work of this group will be to coordinate testing, assess the results, and implement a range of design, content and interaction changes to web pages.

Agility is key: most effective to have people on the team and in design workshops who can approve content changes.

  • project lead:
    • sees the project through to implementation and measurement
    • understands and plans mitigation of the barriers that prevent implementation
  • project coordinator:
    • keeps the team organized, maintains spreadsheets, documentation and schedules meetings
  • design researcher:
    • understands and uncovers user behaviours/expectations
  • content designer:
    • focuses on improvements to language and how content is structured and displayed
  • interaction designer:
    • builds prototypes and provides input on interactive approaches that may enhance the user’s experience

Usability research specialists

Research-driven improvement projects require impartial usability research specialists who can recruit participants and run replicable tests with multiple participants.

This group of people could be in-house or through a third party.

Subject matter experts

Content improvement must be done with the involvement of subject matter experts, who understand the materials to present, and the struggles faced by audiences. These participants may differ, depending on the program area.

  • program and policy experts:
    • depending on the program area, there may be important policy or program information
    • working with program and policy experts can help ensure a thorough understanding of the context and purpose
  • front-line service agents:
    • in cases where your online information supports a “bricks and mortar” service, front-line service agents can be invaluable sources of information about customer needs and frustrations
  • content developers / contributors
  • web support team and relevant Canada.ca theme lead

Senior management and decision makers

Senior management will be required to approve decisions on content, design and interactions. Designing web materials for task success may conflict with organizational pressures. It's critical that senior management be engaged early and often, to understand the methods, and to participate in crafting appropriate project goals.

Some of the greatest risks to project success are the result of a lack of senior management buy-in related to:

  • thinking and designing services from the client’s perspective
  • situations where different directorates may be impacted by changes to service delivery
  • usability testing or analytics evidence contradicting approaches that support competing priorities (political, organizational, regulatory, etc.)
  • overly long approvals processes

Some of the best ways to overcome these risks include:

  • engaging senior management early and often
    • we recommend engaging senior management early and on a regular basis, once a month and then after user testing
  • educating senior management on human-centred service design, evidence gathering approaches and usability testing
  • sharing user stories - especially when audiences struggle with current designs - can be very compelling
  • identifying likely obstacles in advance and tailoring discussions accordingly

Organizational alignment

Improving content for your users often means significant change for organizations. Successful service optimization projects require organizations that are aligned on purpose, approach and objectives.

People shouldn’t need to understand how the government is structured in order to use a government service. To design services for people, we must turn towards a greater understanding of user behaviours, needs and context. To do this, we rely on research and evidence. For many governmental organizations, this is a change from approaches to policy development of program delivery.

How do organizations successfully handle this different approach? We believe it is through aligning how your organization functions to deliver a service more effectively. This means, alignment on:

  • a high-level agreement on what you set out to accomplish through your service improvement
  • buy-in on the approach and the importance of users and evidence in decision-making
  • ensuring you have a project team with the right skills, ready to work together
  • the project lead and their roles and responsibilities
  • clear eyed understanding of the types of barriers to implementation and risks to success you face, with a general plan on how to address them in advance

Effective and intentional governance can address some of the barriers that may get in the way of project success. These barriers include: challenging traditional decision-making and chains of command with new, research-based approaches to service design; ensuring the right people with the right knowledge are involved in service design; and understanding the relationships between service design and technical implementation.

Effective project governance removes ambiguity about who is accountable for which decisions. The United Nations defines “good governance” as the process of decision making and the process by which decisions are implemented (or not implemented).

To establish effective governance, consider the following:

  • Your team should be structured to support the project’s objectives by ensuring there is awareness, acceptance and capacity to produce an outcome that supports your users.
  • This means: ensuring the decision-maker is is in a position to support the implementation of approaches that have been tested and proven to work.
  • To meet objectives, you will need people in roles to be accountable for:
    • implementing the best approaches
    • using evidence to drive decisions
    • engaging with stakeholders effectively
    • understanding and mitigating risk
    • ensuring there is a team-wide understanding of the subject
    • nsuring acceptance of the evidence-based approach among subject matter experts
