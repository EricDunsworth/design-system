Why use a task-based approach

Canada.ca is the Government of Canada primary service delivery channel.

People visit Canada.ca to do things like:

  • check the weather
  • learn about health risks
  • apply for passports
  • pay their taxes
  • receive benefits
  • find jobs
  • learn about product recalls
  • better understand current international trade negotiations

People understand the activities they undertake on Canada.ca as tasks they must complete in order to fulfill their goals or objectives.

Improving services on Canada.ca means ensuring that people are able to complete their tasks as efficiently and easily as possible.

Improving the right thing

You won’t be able to improve everything. Some tasks are much more important to your users – and to your mandate – than others.

To choose what to improve, focus on 3 things:

  • understand what information is most used by your audiences (based on top tasks)
  • identify what is causing the greatest challenges for your users and your business lines (connecting with people dealing with client enquiries in your organizations can be very useful for this)
  • choose what is feasible for you to improve, given time limits, organizational culture and interest levels

Top tasks

Use a people-centred and data-driven way to focus and prioritize improvement efforts on the web by focusing on top tasks.

Choose the top tasks based on how the task currently stands. You can start by looking at top tasks for Canada.ca. Determining what are the top tasks for your organization is crucial to focus on what matters.

Task impact

Determine which of these tasks is causing the greatest struggle for people.

Use the research tools available to you: usability research, web analytics, exit surveys and self-reported task success, direct feedback collected on your pages, data from call centers and mailrooms, public opinion research, etc.

User pain

Get direct feedback from users through moderated usability testing, exit surveys or analytics. Adding the feedback tool directly on your web pages may provide valuable insights.

Call drivers, complaints, web feedback, social media listening

Are you getting more calls, or more complaints from your users about specific services? Is your front-line staff describing areas of struggle? Is there a sudden flurry of posts related to your services on social media? All these may help you focus on what you should work on.

Higher organizational costs

Is a service costly to deliver? Is it possible to lessen the cost by improving online services? Online content that is clearly written and well-structured may mitigate the need for many users to send emails or make phone calls.
